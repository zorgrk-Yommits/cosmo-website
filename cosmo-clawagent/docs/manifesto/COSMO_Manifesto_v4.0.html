COSMO Manifesto v4.0

English Master Version

In one sentence

COSMO is the execution layer for the Agent Economy: a Supra-native system for turning autonomous agent intent into permissioned RFQs, secure settlement, receipts and reputation.

1. Preamble: The next users are not human

The next major users of blockchains will not only be people clicking buttons in wallets. They will be autonomous AI agents acting on behalf of people, teams, protocols, companies and other agents.

Today, we still treat AI as an assistant: something that answers, summarizes, writes and suggests. That is only the first phase. The second phase begins when AI can make decisions, request services, negotiate terms and complete economic actions. At that point, AI no longer only produces information. It participates in markets.

Markets require more than intelligence. They require identity, budgets, permission, reputation, rules, settlement and receipts. Without these, agents may think, but they cannot safely act. They can recommend a trade, but they cannot become accountable economic actors. They can find a service, but they cannot negotiate and settle with it in a trust-minimized way.

COSMO exists for that gap.

2. The thesis: intelligence needs an execution layer

The AI boom has focused on cognition: models, prompts, memory, reasoning and tool use. But an economy is not built from cognition alone. An economy is built from repeatable transactions between actors who can commit, deliver, pay, dispute, improve and be held accountable.

If autonomous agents become real economic participants, they need an execution layer that can translate intent into action. That layer must answer practical questions:

  • Who is allowed to act?
  • What budget can the agent use?
  • Which services can be requested?
  • How are offers compared?
  • How is settlement verified?
  • Who is accountable if execution fails?
  • How does reputation compound over time?

COSMO is built around these questions. It does not try to be the smartest agent. It tries to become the reliable arm that lets agents transact.

3. From chatbots to machine-to-machine commerce

The Agent Economy begins when agents can buy and sell services from each other. This is not science fiction. It is the logical extension of current AI workflows. An agent that writes code may need compute. A research agent may need data. A trading agent may need liquidity. A logistics agent may need storage or routing. A creator agent may need rendering, transcription, voice generation or distribution.

In the human web, discovery led to marketplaces. In the agent web, discovery will lead to programmable RFQs: structured requests for quotes that machines can understand, compare and settle.

A simple example: an AI agent needs GPU capacity for a model run. It asks multiple providers for price, latency, uptime and delivery guarantees. Each provider answers with a quote. The agent checks budget and permissions, selects the best quote and settles automatically. The provider receives payment only under agreed conditions. The result is recorded as a receipt, feeding reputation.

That is not a chatbot. That is commerce.

4. Why blockchain is the natural substrate

Autonomous economic activity cannot rely only on private APIs and promises. Agents need neutral rails where commitments can be verified. They need wallets to hold value, identities to build reputation, rules to constrain behavior and settlement systems that do not disappear when one provider changes terms.

Blockchain gives agents a shared economic substrate. It does not make AI intelligent. It makes AI accountable. It turns actions into transactions, promises into commitments and outcomes into records.

For the Agent Economy, the important question is not whether humans like crypto. The important question is whether autonomous actors need a neutral system for ownership, payment, verification and reputation. We believe they do.

5. Why Supra

Supra is positioned around high-performance execution, automation, cross-chain infrastructure and agent-native systems. That makes it a natural home for an Agent Economy thesis. Autonomous agents need fast finality, programmable execution, reliable data and a stack that can connect reasoning with action.

COSMO chooses Supra because agent commerce should not be bolted onto slow, fragmented infrastructure after the fact. It should live where automation, data and execution can be composed as first-class primitives.

This is also why COSMO must remain honest about integration status. A manifesto is not a signed partnership agreement. COSMO is designed to complement SupraOS and SupraFX. Final integration surfaces depend on access, alignment and technical feasibility.

6. What COSMO is

COSMO is an execution and accountability layer for autonomous agent workflows on Supra. It is designed to help agents turn intent into permissioned RFQ execution and verifiable settlement.

At its core, COSMO explores a simple loop:

  1. An agent expresses intent: I need X under these constraints.
  2. A request for quote is created with budget, assets, deadline and rules.
  3. Makers or service providers respond with quotes.
  4. The taker chooses a quote and funds or authorizes execution.
  5. Settlement is executed under defined conditions.
  6. The outcome becomes a receipt and feeds reputation.

This loop can serve DeFi, but it is not limited to DeFi. It can serve compute, data, APIs, storage, agent-to-agent services and any environment where autonomous actors need to request, compare and settle work.

7. What COSMO is not

COSMO is not another chatbot. It is not a general-purpose AI model. It is not a proprietary bridge. It is not an AMM. It is not a promise that one protocol will replace the rest of the Supra ecosystem.

Most importantly, COSMO is not a competitor to SupraFX.

SupraFX can be understood as market-facing infrastructure: liquidity, trading, routing and cross-chain execution surfaces. COSMO is focused on the agent-side execution contract: permissioning, RFQ intent, accountability, settlement receipts and operator reputation. In a mature stack, these are complementary. Agent intent can flow through COSMO and execute through routes or venues such as SupraFX where appropriate.

Positioning principle

SupraOS coordinates agents. SupraFX provides market and liquidity rails. COSMO turns autonomous intent into accountable execution. The winning story is not competition. It is composition.

9. RFQ as the native language of agent commerce

Humans negotiate in conversations. Machines need structured commitments. RFQ is one of the cleanest primitives for that world because it separates intent, quote, funding, execution and settlement.

RFQ gives agents a way to ask: Who can provide this service? At what price? Under which constraints? Until what deadline? With what minimum acceptable outcome? This format is flexible enough for token swaps, compute requests, data access, storage, model inference and agent services.

COSMO treats RFQ not merely as a trading mechanism, but as a general coordination primitive for autonomous economic actors.

10. Accountability: the difference between automation and agency

Automation without accountability is dangerous. It creates systems that act quickly but cannot be trusted. True agency requires limits. It requires budgets, permissions, bonds, logs and consequences.

COSMO therefore focuses on the accountability layer: who can quote, who can execute, which assets are allowed, how much can be used, what happens when a leg fails and how operator quality is reflected over time.

This is where $COSMO matters. A token in this context is not a slogan. It is a way to bind participants to the quality of execution. Operators should have something at stake. Holders should have a role in access and coordination. Reputation should be earned through performance, not claimed through marketing.

11. Proof matters

The Agent Economy is full of words. COSMO must be judged by proof. As of July 2026, COSMO runs on Supra mainnet in a deliberately guarded configuration, and the core loop of this manifesto is no longer a diagram. It has happened on-chain:

  • Live RFQ settlement on mainnet. The RFQ engine, maker-vault and bond model are deployed and operating, with parameters governed by a multi-signature admin rather than a single key.
  • The first community maker. An independent operator posted its own bond, quoted through the permissioned quote path and completed a full request-to-settlement roundtrip on mainnet. Every leg is publicly documented with transaction links on the demo page. Maker capacity opens one bonded slot at a time under global caps: accountable growth instead of an open faucet.
  • The first compute job. The GPU/compute use case from this manifesto is live as an outcome-based RFQ market: the first real job was requested with escrowed payment, delivered against a verifiable result hash, and settled on-chain end-to-end, with the provider operating under a slashable bond. Anti-spam request pricing is active.
  • Operations, not promises. The market runs with automated keeper infrastructure, on-chain invariant monitoring and public evidence documents for every launch step.

These proofs do not mean the system is finished. Caps are intentionally small, quality gates are intentionally strict, and every step of the opening is documented. A primitive is enough to start building a market around it. The next challenge is not to invent new slogans, but to connect real agents, real makers, real services and real demand.

12. Use cases that make the thesis concrete

  • GPU and compute: agents request compute capacity, compare quotes and pay for completed jobs. This market is live in its first guarded version.
  • APIs and data: agents purchase weather, market, logistics, identity or research data through metered access.
  • Storage and bandwidth: agents allocate files, pay providers and verify delivery receipts.
  • DeFi execution: agents route capital, request quotes and settle under defined risk rules.
  • Agent-to-agent services: specialized agents sell analysis, code, monitoring, translation or model inference to other agents.
  • Community markets: holders and operators create service supply for agent workflows instead of only speculating on tokens.

The common pattern is not the asset. The common pattern is the transaction: request, quote, permission, execution, settlement, receipt, reputation.

13. The role of the community

COSMO should not become a black box operated by a small team. The strongest version of COSMO is a community-built operator economy. Makers provide liquidity, services or execution capacity. Builders integrate new agent workflows. Holders become early participants in governance, access and reputation. Content creators translate the thesis for new audiences.

The community is not only a marketing channel. It is the first market. It is where agent services can be imagined, tested, priced and improved before the wider world understands what is happening.

Märkte brauchen mehr als Intelligenz. Sie brauchen Identität, Budgets, Berechtigungen, Reputation, Regeln, Settlement und Nachweise. Ohne diese Bausteine können Agenten denken, aber nicht sicher handeln.

2. Die These: Intelligenz braucht eine Ausführungsschicht

Der aktuelle KI-Boom konzentriert sich auf Denken: Modelle, Prompts, Memory, Reasoning und Tools. Eine Wirtschaft entsteht aber nicht aus Denken allein. Eine Wirtschaft entsteht aus wiederholbaren Transaktionen zwischen Akteuren, die zusagen, liefern, bezahlen, streiten, lernen und verantwortlich gemacht werden können.

Wenn autonome Agenten echte wirtschaftliche Teilnehmer werden, brauchen sie eine Execution Layer, die Absicht in Handlung übersetzt. Diese Schicht muss praktische Fragen beantworten:

  • Wer darf handeln?
  • Welches Budget darf der Agent nutzen?
  • Welche Services dürfen angefragt werden?
  • Wie werden Angebote verglichen?
  • Wie wird Settlement verifiziert?

  • Wie entsteht Reputation über Zeit?

COSMO versucht nicht, der intelligenteste Agent zu sein. COSMO versucht, der zuverlässige Arm zu sein, mit dem Agenten sicher wirtschaftlich handeln können.

3. Von Chatbots zu Machine-to-Machine Commerce

Die Agent Economy beginnt, wenn Agenten Dienstleistungen voneinander kaufen und verkaufen können. Ein Agent, der Code schreibt, braucht Compute. Ein Research-Agent braucht Daten. Ein Trading-Agent braucht Liquidität. Ein Creator-Agent braucht Rendering, Transkription, Voice oder Distribution.

Im menschlichen Internet führte Discovery zu Marktplätzen. Im Agenten-Internet führt Discovery zu programmierbaren RFQs: strukturierten Angebotsanfragen, die Maschinen verstehen, vergleichen und abwickeln können.

Ein einfaches Beispiel: Ein KI-Agent benötigt GPU-Leistung für einen Modelllauf. Er fragt mehrere Anbieter nach Preis, Latenz, Verfügbarkeit und Liefergarantie. Die Anbieter antworten mit Angeboten. Der Agent prüft Budget und Berechtigungen, wählt das beste Angebot und bezahlt automatisch. Das Ergebnis wird als Nachweis gespeichert und fließt in Reputation ein.

Das ist kein Chatbot. Das ist Handel.

4. Warum Blockchain die natürliche Grundlage ist

Autonome wirtschaftliche Aktivität kann sich nicht nur auf private APIs und Versprechen verlassen. Agenten brauchen neutrale Schienen, auf denen Zusagen überprüfbar sind. Sie brauchen Wallets, Identitäten, Regeln und Systeme, die Settlement nachvollziehbar machen.

Blockchain gibt Agenten eine gemeinsame wirtschaftliche Grundlage. Sie macht KI nicht intelligenter. Sie macht KI verantwortlich. Sie macht aus Aktionen Transaktionen, aus Versprechen Verpflichtungen und aus Ergebnissen Nachweise.

6. Was COSMO ist

COSMO ist eine Execution- und Accountability-Layer für autonome Agenten-Workflows auf Supra. COSMO soll helfen, Agentenabsichten in berechtigte RFQ-Ausführung und verifizierbares Settlement zu übersetzen.

Der Kern ist ein einfacher Ablauf:

  1. Ein Agent formuliert eine Absicht: Ich brauche X unter diesen Bedingungen.
  2. Eine RFQ wird mit Budget, Assets, Deadline und Regeln erstellt.
  3. Maker oder Service-Anbieter antworten mit Angeboten.
  4. Der Taker wählt ein Angebot und autorisiert die Ausführung.
  5. Das Settlement wird unter definierten Bedingungen ausgeführt.
  6. Das Ergebnis wird als Nachweis gespeichert und stärkt oder schwächt Reputation.

Dieser Ablauf kann DeFi bedienen, ist aber nicht auf DeFi beschränkt. Er kann Compute, Daten, APIs, Storage und Agent-to-Agent Services bedienen.

9. RFQ als Sprache der Agent Economy

Menschen verhandeln in Gesprächen. Maschinen brauchen strukturierte Zusagen. RFQ ist dafür ein sauberer Grundbaustein, weil es Absicht, Angebot, Finanzierung, Ausführung und Settlement trennt.

RFQ erlaubt Agenten zu fragen: Wer kann diese Leistung liefern? Zu welchem Preis? Unter welchen Bedingungen? Bis wann? Mit welchem Mindestresultat? Dieses Format passt nicht nur zu Token-Swaps, sondern auch zu Compute, Daten, APIs, Storage und Agent Services.

10. Accountability: der Unterschied zwischen Automation und Agency

Automation ohne Verantwortung ist gefährlich. Sie erzeugt Systeme, die schnell handeln, aber nicht vertrauenswürdig sind. Echte Agency braucht Grenzen: Budgets, Berechtigungen, Bonds, Logs und Konsequenzen.

Deshalb fokussiert COSMO die Accountability-Layer. Wer darf quoten? Wer darf ausführen? Welche Assets sind erlaubt? Wie hoch ist das Limit? Was passiert, wenn eine Ausführung fehlschlägt? Wie bildet sich Qualität über Zeit ab?

$COSMO ist in diesem Kontext kein reiner Slogan. Der Token soll Teilnehmer an die Qualität der Ausführung binden. Operator müssen etwas riskieren. Holder sollen Zugang und Koordination ermöglichen. Reputation muss durch Performance verdient werden.
